Question presented

Located more than one thousand miles off the coast of mainland United States, defendants who reside in the Virgin Islands may struggle to collect awarded costs against off-island plaintiffs. Cognizant of this potential burden, the Virgin Islands legislature enacted Section 547, which mandates: [i]f the plaintiff resides out of the Virgin Islands or is a foreign corporation, the defendant may serve a notice requiring security for the costs which may be awarded against the plaintiff. After the service of such a notice, all proceedings in the action shall be stayed until security is given by the plaintiff. 5 V.I.C. § 547(a). The question presented is: Whether a statute requiring a court, upon a defendant’s request, to order a non-resident plaintiff to post a minimal security bond for costs violates either the Privileges and Immunities Clause of Article IV, Section 2 of the United States Constitution or the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.
