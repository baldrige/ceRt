Question presented

Petitioner plaintiff Peter Brownstein is the coauthor of the LCID, a “joint work” first fixed in 1994 and licensed for a 20-year term to defendant-respondent defendant Ethnic Technologies (“ET”), and used to create a further derivative work known as the E-Tech. The LCID analyzed bulk name and address lists and drew conclusions about individuals’ ethnicity, language and religion, a process referred to as encoding. Brownstein wrote the original mainframe computer programs to automate the encoding process, which yielded the first commercially viable product The principal issue raised here is the transformative nature of a joint work under the Copyright Act, creating a single unified whole from its parts; as opposed to the creation of derivative works that add to an existing work, but which are not integrated into a single work. A joint work under the Copyright Act creates a single, indivisible work.
