Question presented

William Johnson assaulted the mother of his child, causing her to black out. Johnson also took the victim’s phone. When the victim regained consciousness, she immediately fled from her apartment with her child to her parent's house. There, she called 911. The victim identified her child's father as the man who harmed her. She did this in response to the dispatcher’s question, "Where's the emergency?" She said there was a gun in her apartment. Even though she reported being “choked” and having her head banged against the wall, she declined medical treatment and expressed uncertainty about having charges against Johnson. The victim did not testify at Johnson’s criminal trial. The judge admitted the 911 recording into evidence despite a defense objection. The State argued the 911 call's primary purpose was nontestimonial and that the victim’s statements were excited utterances made during an ongoing emergency. The question presented under the Confrontation Clause of the Sixth Amendment is: When viewed objectively, were the statements describing the fresh incident of domestic violence in the 911 recording non-testimonial?
