Question presented

In a civil rights action based on excessive force, a deputy sheriff denies engaging in the physical acts that constituted excessive force. The plaintiff at trial proves that the deputy’s denials were false and that he did engage in the acts. The jury determines that the deputy used excessive force and did so with malice, oppression or recklessness. Is the deputy entitled to qualified immunity for engaging in the acts of excessive force, despite his denials of having done so and the jury’s findings?
