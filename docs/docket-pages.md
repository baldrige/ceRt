# Docket pages (`/cases/`)

Per-case pages published under `gh-pages:cases/<docket>.html` — one per Supreme
Court docket across every term the pipeline has fetched (currently OT2017–present,
~55k pages). Generated by `R/docket_page.R`. Served at
`https://supremecourt.report/cases/<docket>.html`.

## Rendering pipeline

- `render_dockets_for(cases, site_dir)` — CI entry point. Loads the cert models,
  the on-site QP caches, and the Rule 10 signal caches, then calls
  `render_docket_pages()`. Wrapped in `tryCatch` (never fatal to a build).
- `render_docket_pages(cases, out_dir, …)` — one page per row of `cases`.
  **Incremental**: `cases/.manifest.json` holds a per-docket content hash
  `digest(list(PAGE_TEMPLATE_VERSION, model_id, caption, events, parties, …))`; a
  docket re-renders only when that hash changes. The manifest is *merged*, not
  overwritten, so rendering one term never wipes another's entries from the shared
  `/cases/`.
- `docket_page(cx, …)` — builds one page: disposition/forecast box, Questions
  Presented, counsel, related cases, and the proceedings timeline.
- `write_search_index()` — maintains `cases/search.json` (docket → caption) for the
  home-page search box; accumulated across all batches.

### `PAGE_TEMPLATE_VERSION`

A string constant near the top of `docket_page.R`. **Bump it whenever the
markup/CSS/coloring logic changes** — it is part of every page's content hash, so a
bump invalidates the whole manifest and forces every page to re-render. This is a
*render-only* change: no re-fetch of docket data is required (see "Rolling a
template change to the back-catalog" below). The version history is kept in the
code header comment (v5 … v12).

## Brief-cover timeline dots (Rule 33.1(g))

Each proceedings-timeline dot is tinted to the Court's booklet-cover color for that
filing. Classification is `brief_cover(text, granted_on, entry_date, resp_brief_on)`,
driven purely by the docket's "Proceedings and Orders" phrasing:

| filing | color |
| --- | --- |
| Petition for certiorari (jurisdictional statement / extraordinary writ) | white |
| Brief in opposition (motion to dismiss or affirm) | orange |
| Amicus, petition stage | cream |
| Petitioner's brief on the merits | blue |
| Respondent's brief on the merits | red |
| Reply brief on the merits | yellow |
| Reply to brief in opposition / supplemental / rehearing | tan |
| Amicus on the merits, for petitioner **or neither party** | light green |
| Amicus on the merits, for respondent | dark green |
| Everything procedural (orders, applications, motions, waivers, argument) | hollow |

A compact legend renders under the "Proceedings" heading **only** on pages that
carry at least one colored dot. Colors are defined as CSS custom properties in
`DOCKET_CSS` and applied per entry via an inline `--dot` variable.

### Cert-stage vs merits split — `granted_on`

`granted_on` is the cert-grant date, detected from the docket. Entries before it
keep the safe cert-stage covers (e.g. amicus = cream); entries on/after it get the
merits covers (blue/red/green). Detection excludes **stay/conditional orders** —
their boilerplate ("in the event the petition … is granted", "pending disposition",
"for a stay") would otherwise read as a grant and back-date the merits stage.

### Amicus side — Rule 37 timing (the subtle part)

SCOTUS **usually omits "in support of petitioner/respondent" from the docket line**
(it lives in the PDF, not the proceeding text), so the side cannot be read directly
in ~97% of cases. Instead it is inferred from Supreme Court **Rule 37** deadlines:

- Amici **for petitioner or neither party** are due 7 days after the *petitioner's*
  merits brief.
- Amici **for respondent** are due 7 days after the *respondent's* merits brief.

So a merits amicus filed **on/after the respondent's merits-brief date → dark
green**; earlier → light green. An explicit "in support of …" in the text (rare)
still overrides the timing.

`resp_brief_on` = the **latest** "Brief of/for respondent" on/after the grant,
excluding the cert-stage "in opposition" and any supplemental brief. **Latest, not
earliest**: a respondent aligned *with* the petitioner — e.g. private plaintiffs
when the United States is petitioner (**23-477, US v. Skrmetti**) — files on the
petitioner's *earlier* schedule, so only the last "Brief of respondent" marks the
party actually opposing the petitioner. Single-respondent cases are unaffected
(min == max).

> Validation cases: **17-965** (Trump v. Hawaii) and **25-332** (Trump v. Slaughter)
> — clean single boundary; **23-477** (US v. Skrmetti) — the cross-aligned case that
> motivated the latest-brief rule.

## Fetch model (`R/scotus_dash_new.R`)

- **No persistent per-docket cache.** `fetch_cases(dkts)` re-hits supremecourt.gov
  one docket at a time (`fetch_case_result`, which retries transient throttling).
  SCOTUS's Akamai WAF throttles bursty/sustained clients (403), so fetches are
  sequential, and each term runs on its own runner (fresh IP).
- **Docket buckets per term:** paid `NN-1..`, IFP `NN-5001..`, applications
  `NNA1..`. `binary_search_max()` finds each bucket's highest live number.
  ⚠️ Application dockets use the form `NNA###` (an "A"), **not** a dash — code that
  scans/globs docket pages must match `[-A]`, not just `-`.
- `get_scotus_update(year)` — the **daily** fetch: only the **trailing ~50 dockets
  of each bucket**. This is why the daily refreshes only the ~150 most-recent
  current-term pages and why a template change does *not* reach older pages on its
  own.
- `get_scotus_term(year)` — the **full term** (every docket in each bucket);
  thousands of requests. Used by the backfill/fill workflows.
- `fetch_is_degraded(ot, tol = 0.1)` — refuse to publish if >10% of attempted
  dockets failed (throttling). The daily `quit(status = 0)`s and leaves the site
  unchanged; per-term fetch jobs `quit(status = 1)` so a partial term never
  publishes.

## Rolling a template change to the back-catalog

The daily only rebuilds the current term's ~150 recent pages, so after a
`PAGE_TEMPLATE_VERSION` bump the rest of the ~55k pages must be re-rendered
explicitly. Two dispatchable workflows do this:

### `rerender-dockets.yml` — full re-render

- **Fetch mode** (default): a per-term matrix full-fetches each term on its own
  runner (`fetch_term.R` → `cases-<term>.rds` artifact), then a **single** render
  job renders every term into gh-pages and commits **once** (one commit avoids the
  manifest / `search.json` races that concurrent per-term commits would hit).
- **`reuse_from_runs` input** — render-only: skip fetch, pull the `cases-*.rds`
  snapshots from the named prior run(s) via `gh run download`, then render. Use this
  for a pure template bump (no data change): ~20 min instead of a ~3 h re-scrape.
  (Reusing multiple runs works only if their artifact names don't collide — the
  flatten step keeps one file per name.)
- Rendering is incremental, so a partly-throttled or interrupted run re-dispatches
  cheaply — already-current pages skip.

### `fill-throttled-dockets.yml` — mop up stragglers

A full fetch loses a small fraction of dockets to throttling; those keep a **stale
page** (see below). This workflow fetches **only** the stragglers — far fewer than a
whole term, so far less throttling — then renders them. Shares the backfill's
concurrency lane so the two never race on gh-pages. Two modes:

- **SCAN (default)** — per term (`terms` input, blank = 17–25), scans the published
  site for stale pages and fetches just those docket numbers. Re-dispatch until
  every term reports zero stale pages.
- **EXPLICIT** — pass `dockets` (comma-separated, any terms, e.g.
  `17-7925,19-206`) to fetch exactly that set, bypassing the scan. This is the
  surgical path for a handful of dockets that survived several scan passes (lost to
  throttling *every* time). Internally it runs one `manual` job driven by the
  `DOCKETS` env; the render step picks up its `cases-manual.rds` like any other
  snapshot.

**Identifying a stale page** (`fetch_missing_dockets.R`), two signals:

1. **Pre-template (pre-v8):** the template tags every timeline entry as
   `<li class='proc'>` or `<li style='--dot:…'>`; a **bare `<li>`** marks a
   pre-template render. Anchor the check to `class='timeline'><li>` — a
   multi-question Questions-Presented list *also* contains bare `<li>` items and
   would otherwise read as a false positive.
2. **Version-behind (v12+):** each page stamps its template version in the head as
   `<meta name='tv' content='vNN'>`. A page whose stamp is **older than the current
   `PAGE_TEMPLATE_VERSION`** was left behind by a bump (a throttle casualty keeps
   its old stamp). **Unstamped pages are grandfathered** — every pre-v12 page lacks
   the stamp, and flagging them would re-render the whole back-catalog; they remain
   reachable only via the bare-`<li>` rule or an explicit `dockets` list.
   *Bootstrap:* version-detection only catches a docket once it has been stamped at
   least once, so its coverage grows as pages re-render (the stamp rides along on
   any future full rollout — no dedicated pass needed).

The scan matches application dockets (`NNA###`) too, not just `NN-###`.

## Publishing

Every page lives on the **`gh-pages`** branch (GitHub Pages → supremecourt.report).
Workflows check out gh-pages into `./site`, render, then commit and push with a
**rebase-and-retry** loop, because the daily / conferences / backfill workflows can
all push to gh-pages concurrently. Each publish re-asserts the `CNAME`
(`supremecourt.report`) so a rebuild can't drop the custom domain. Pages typically
redeploys within ~1–2 min of a push; a viewer may see a cached copy for up to the
`max-age=600` (10 min) CDN window (hard-refresh to bypass).
